How to Solve Star Battle Puzzles

Star Battle hints work best when they point you toward a forced placement, not a guess. The core question is always the same: which cells can still hold a star after the row, column, region, and no-touch rules are applied?

Use this guide when you are stuck on a daily Star Battle puzzle, a Queens-style puzzle, or a Two Not Touch variant. The examples use one-star-per-row puzzles like the daily boards on Daily Logic Lab, but the same thinking scales to larger Star Battle grids.

Quick Star Battle Solving Checklist

  1. Scan every row, column, and color region for groups with very few legal cells.
  2. Place a star only when a group has one possible cell left.
  3. After placing a star, mark all touching cells as impossible.
  4. Also remove the rest of that star's row, column, and region.
  5. Repeat the scan. Each forced star should make the next step easier.

Hint 1: Start With the Tightest Region

A color region is often the best place to begin because it has an unusual shape. A long, thin, corner-heavy, or broken-looking region may only have two or three cells that can still hold a star. Compare those cells against the row and column rules.

If every candidate but one is blocked by an existing star, by a row that already has a star, or by the no-touch rule, the remaining cell is forced. This is stronger than a hunch: the region must contain one star, and there is only one legal place left.

Hint 2: Mark Around Every Star

The no-touch rule is easy to underuse. When a star is placed, the eight surrounding squares become impossible, including diagonal neighbors. Then the rest of the star's row, column, and color region become impossible too.

This marking step is where many Star Battle puzzles open up. A cell that looked possible a moment ago may be touching a new star. A row that had three candidates may suddenly have one. Good solvers do not just place stars; they harvest every elimination that the star creates.

Hint 3: Look for Only-One-Place Groups

Rows, columns, and regions all need exactly one star. When one of those groups has only one legal cell left, place the star there. This is the simplest Star Battle strategy, and it is also the most reliable.

The trick is to count legal cells, not empty cells. A square is not legal if it touches a star, sits in a row that already has a star, sits in a column that already has a star, or belongs to a region that already has a star.

Hint 4: Use Rows and Columns Together

Sometimes a region has two possible cells, but both are in the same row. That means the star for that region must use that row, even if you do not yet know which of the two cells is correct. You can often remove other candidates from the same row in nearby regions.

The same idea works with columns. If all possible cells for a region sit in one column, that region reserves the column. These locked-row and locked-column patterns are useful when the puzzle does not give you an immediate forced star.

Worked Example: What to Do When Stuck

Suppose a row has three empty cells left: A, B, and C. Cell A touches an existing star, so it cannot hold a star. Cell B is in a color region that already has a star. Cell C is the only legal cell left in the row. You can place a star in C, then mark every touching cell around C as impossible.

Now check what changed. The column containing C is solved, so every other cell in that column is impossible. The color region containing C is solved too. Those eliminations may leave another row, column, or region with only one possible star cell. This chain reaction is the normal rhythm of a Star Battle solve.

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting diagonal touching. Diagonal stars are illegal.
  • Counting empty cells instead of legal cells.
  • Placing a star because it feels likely instead of proving it is forced.
  • Ignoring color regions after focusing on rows and columns.
  • Not revisiting earlier groups after a new star creates fresh eliminations.

FAQ

What is the best first hint for Star Battle?

Start with the tightest row, column, or color region. The best first move is usually a group with only one or two legal cells.

How do I know a star is forced?

A star is forced when a row, column, or region needs a star and every other cell in that group is illegal.

Can stars touch diagonally?

No. Stars cannot touch horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.

Should I guess if I am stuck?

Try the checklist first. A solver-verified puzzle should have a logical path, even if the next constrained group is easy to miss.

What is the difference between Star Battle and Queens?

Both use row, column, and region placement logic. Star Battle adds the no-touch rule, which makes nearby cells much more important.
