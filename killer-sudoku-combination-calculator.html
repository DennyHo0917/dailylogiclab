Worked Example: 17 in 3 Cells

A 3-cell cage with a sum of 17 and no repeated digits has seven basic combinations: 1 + 7 + 9, 2 + 6 + 9, 2 + 7 + 8, 3 + 5 + 9, 3 + 6 + 8, 4 + 5 + 8, and 4 + 6 + 7. If one of those digits is already used in the same row, column, or box, remove it from the available digits and the list gets shorter.

This is the main value of a cage calculator: it does the arithmetic quickly, but the solving still comes from Sudoku logic. The output tells you what can fit the cage; the grid tells you which of those combinations can actually survive.

Killer Sudoku, Kakuro, and KenKen

Killer Sudoku usually combines Sudoku placement rules with cage sums, so a digit cannot repeat inside a cage. Kakuro also uses clue sums and combinations, but the layout is different. KenKen and other arithmetic puzzles may allow or disallow repeats depending on the publisher. That is why the repeat toggle is included here: the same search is useful across several puzzle families, but the rule has to match the puzzle in front of you.

FAQ

What does a Killer Sudoku combination calculator do?

It lists the possible digit sets for a cage total and cell count, then lets you remove unavailable digits.

Should I allow repeated digits?

Leave repeats off for standard Killer Sudoku. Turn repeats on only for puzzle variants that explicitly allow duplicate digits in a cage.

Why is the maximum sum higher than 45?

No-repeat Sudoku digits 1 through 9 total 45, but repeat-friendly variants can exceed that when a cage has many cells.

Can this solve the full Killer Sudoku grid?

No. It only finds cage combinations. You still use Sudoku logic, row and column restrictions, boxes, and cage placement to solve the grid.
